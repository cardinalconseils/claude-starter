# Positioning Skill

Positioning is not a tagline, a slogan, or a brand promise. Positioning is the decision about which context makes your product obviously the right choice for the right buyer. Everything downstream — messaging, copy, campaigns, community — depends on positioning being correct first.

## The Two-Layer Stack

**Layer 1 — Positioning (April Dunford):** How does this product fit into the market? What category does it win in? For whom is it obviously the best choice?

**Layer 2 — Brand philosophy (Seth Godin):** What does this brand believe? Who does it lead? What story does the customer tell themselves?

Neither layer substitutes for the other. Positioning without philosophy produces market fit without loyalty. Philosophy without positioning produces tribe without conversion.

## Layer 1: April Dunford's Positioning Framework

### The 5 Components of Positioning

Positioning has exactly 5 components. All 5 must be defined. Partially defined positioning is worse than no positioning — it gives teams false confidence.

**1. Competitive Alternatives**
What would the customer do if this product didn't exist? This is not the feature-by-feature competitor list. It's the actual alternatives: Excel, hiring someone, doing nothing, a completely different category of solution.

The test: if a customer churned tomorrow, what would they use instead?

**2. Unique Attributes**
What does this product have that the competitive alternatives genuinely lack? These must be:
- Specific (not "better support" — "24/7 live chat with <2 min response time")
- Verifiable (you can prove it)
- Differentiating (the alternatives don't have it)

**3. Value from Attributes**
For each unique attribute: what outcome does it enable? The chain: attribute → capability → customer outcome.

Example: `24/7 live chat` → `can resolve issues at 2am` → `never misses a time-sensitive deal because of a software blocker`

**4. Best-Fit Customer Characteristics**
Who benefits most from the value in Component 3? Not all customers gain equally. The best-fit customer profile is defined behaviorally: what situation are they in? What do they value? What alternatives do they currently use?

**5. Positioning Statement**
Assembled from the above:
```
For [best-fit customers] who [situation/need],
[product] is the [category frame]
that [unique value].
Unlike [competitive alternatives],
[product] [key differentiator].
```

### Positioning Stress-Tests

After drafting the positioning statement, run these tests:

| Test | Pass condition |
|------|---------------|
| The swap test | Swap in a direct competitor's name — does the statement still work? (If yes, the differentiation is too weak.) |
| The alternative test | Name the top 3 competitive alternatives — does the positioning clearly explain why this wins against each? |
| The best-fit test | Name 3 best-fit customers — would they read the positioning and say "that's exactly what I need"? |
| The sales test | Show the positioning statement to a salesperson — do they say "I can work with this"? |

## Layer 2: Seth Godin's Brand Philosophy

### Smallest Viable Audience

The instinct is to target everyone. The correct move is to find the smallest audience that would truly miss the brand if it disappeared. Build for them obsessively. This creates the signal that attracts the broader audience organically.

Smallest viable audience definition checklist:
- [ ] Can you describe 10 specific people who fit this profile?
- [ ] Do they have a shared worldview — things they believe that others don't?
- [ ] Does the brand say something they've wanted someone to say?
- [ ] Would they tell others about this brand without being asked?

### The Brand Promise

A brand promise is not a tagline. It is the one thing a customer is guaranteed to get every time they interact with this brand. It is about consistency, not aspiration.

The test: Does every customer touchpoint — the product, the support, the content, the sales process — deliver this promise? If not, the brand has an operations problem, not a messaging problem.

### The Story the Customer Tells Themselves

When someone buys this product, what does it say about them? Every purchase is partly a self-definition. Positioning gets the rational side right. Brand philosophy gets the identity side right.

Questions to surface the self-story:
- "When you use this product, what kind of person does that make you?"
- "What would your peers think if you recommended this?"
- "What does using this instead of [alternative] say about your priorities?"

### The Purple Cow

Remarkability is not optional. Advertising a mediocre product makes it fail faster. The question before any campaign: is there anything here worth remarking on?

If yes: build the campaign around it.
If no: the brand strategy work must happen before the campaign work.

### Generosity Strategy

The brand that gives the most away — knowledge, tools, perspective — earns the most trust. This is not a content marketing tactic. It is a positioning choice: the brand is on the side of the customer, not extracting from them.

Generosity strategy definition:
- What category knowledge does this brand have that competitors charge for?
- What can the brand teach that makes customers better at their jobs (not just better at using the product)?
- What community resource can the brand create that would exist even if the product didn't?

## Community Development Framework

Community is a brand's most defensible asset. It cannot be purchased, copied, or acquired. It is built by leading a tribe that shares a worldview.

**Phase 1 — Define the tribe**
Who belongs? Not "our customers." The people who share the belief that makes this brand exist. What do they believe that most people don't say out loud?

**Phase 2 — Create the minimum viable community**
One place where the tribe gathers. Not five channels — one. Forum, Slack, newsletter, LinkedIn group. Wherever the tribe already congregates.

**Phase 3 — Lead with agenda, not promotion**
The brand's role in the community is teacher and curator, not promoter. Every touchpoint should inform, challenge, or celebrate the tribe — not sell to them.

**Phase 4 — Give members status**
The community grows when members gain recognition within it. Spotlight customers, create contribution pathways, let community members lead.

## Key Messages Architecture

After positioning and brand philosophy are defined, key messages are assembled:

```yaml
primary_claim: "[single sentence that captures the positioned value]"
support_points:
  - claim: "[support point 1]"
    proof: "[specific evidence: data, case study, quote]"
  - claim: "[support point 2]"
    proof: "[specific evidence]"
  - claim: "[support point 3]"
    proof: "[specific evidence]"
objections:
  - objection: "[top customer objection]"
    response: "[positioning-grounded response]"
segments:
  - segment: "[ICP 1]"
    tailored_claim: "[how primary claim is reframed for this segment]"
```

## Value Proposition Canvas

The canvas maps the positioning to customer reality:

**Customer side:**
- Jobs: what the customer is trying to get done (functional, emotional, social)
- Pains: obstacles, risks, bad outcomes — ranked by severity
- Gains: desired outcomes, what success looks like — ranked by importance

**Product side:**
- Pain relievers: how the product alleviates each pain
- Gain creators: how the product enables each desired outcome

The positioning is correct when the pain relievers map to the highest-severity pains and the gain creators map to the highest-importance gains.

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Our positioning is our tagline" | A tagline is the output of positioning, not the positioning itself. |
| "We position differently to different segments" | You have one positioning. You may have segment-specific messaging derived from it. |
| "The competitive alternatives are just the named competitors" | What the customer would do if the product didn't exist often isn't the obvious competitor. |
| "Positioning is marketing's job" | Positioning is a company-wide decision. Sales, product, and marketing must all live in it. |
| "We'll figure out community later" | Community built during growth takes years. Community built after decline is damage control. |
