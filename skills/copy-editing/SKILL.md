# Copy Editing

Expert knowledge for sharpening, clarifying, and improving marketing copy — from a single sentence to a full landing page.

## Core Principle

Good copy editing removes everything that doesn't earn its place. Every word must either carry meaning or carry rhythm. Cut the rest.

## The Edit Hierarchy

Apply these edits in order. Each layer builds on the previous:

1. **Clarity edit** — Can a reader understand this on the first pass?
2. **Benefit edit** — Is the customer's outcome front and center?
3. **Concision edit** — What can be cut without losing meaning?
4. **Voice edit** — Does this sound like a human talking to a human?
5. **CTA edit** — Is the action crystal clear?

## 1. Clarity Edit

**Common clarity killers:**

| Problem | Example | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Jargon | "Leverage our synergistic platform" | "Use one tool for everything" |
| Passive voice | "Results are delivered by our team" | "Our team delivers results" |
| Nominalization | "Make an improvement" | "Improve" |
| Abstract nouns | "Enable optimization of workflows" | "Help you optimize workflows" |
| Nested clauses | "The feature, which was released last year, that customers use most..." | "Customers use this feature most. We released it last year." |

**Clarity test:** Read it out loud. If you stumble, the reader will too.

**Fog Index check:** Count average syllables per word and words per sentence. If average sentence length > 20 words, split sentences.

## 2. Benefit Edit

Features describe the product. Benefits describe what the customer gets.

**Feature → Benefit conversion:**
- "Real-time sync" → "Your team always works from the same data"
- "99.9% uptime" → "Never lose access when you need it most"
- "Automated reports" → "Save 3 hours every Monday morning"
- "Role-based permissions" → "Control who sees what without IT involvement"

**Benefit editing process:**
1. Identify every feature claim in the copy
2. Ask: "So what? What does this mean for the customer?"
3. Rewrite as a customer outcome

**Lead with benefits, follow with features.** Features are proof that the benefit is real. They should appear after the benefit, not before.

## 3. Concision Edit

**Cut these immediately:**

| Phrase | Replace with |
|---|---|
| "In order to" | "To" |
| "Due to the fact that" | "Because" |
| "At this point in time" | "Now" |
| "In the event that" | "If" |
| "The fact that" | (cut entirely) |
| "It is important to note that" | (cut — just say the thing) |
| "We believe that" | (cut — just say what you believe) |
| "Utilize" | "Use" |
| "Facilitate" | "Help" |
| "Leverage" | "Use" |
| "Solution" | (name the specific thing) |
| "World-class" | (cut — says nothing) |
| "Best-in-class" | (cut — prove it instead) |
| "Seamless" | (cut — everyone claims this) |

**Cut filler words:**
- "Really," "very," "quite," "rather," "somewhat," "essentially," "basically," "actually," "certainly"

**Concision test:** Remove a word. Does the sentence still mean the same thing? If yes, cut the word.

## 4. Voice Edit

**Signs of corporate voice (bad):**
- Third person about the company ("Acme provides...")
- Passive everywhere
- No contractions
- Overly formal vocabulary
- No specific people, numbers, or examples

**Signs of human voice (good):**
- Second person to the reader ("You can...")
- Active verbs
- Contractions ("We'll", "you're", "it's")
- Specific numbers ("3 hours" not "significant time")
- Conversational sentence starters ("Here's the thing..." "The problem is...")

**Voice consistency check:**
- Does this sound like one person wrote it?
- Does it match the company's brand voice guidelines?
- Would the target reader recognize their own language?

## 5. CTA Edit

**Weak CTAs:**
- "Submit"
- "Click here"
- "Learn more"
- "Get started"

**Strong CTAs:**
- "Start your free 14-day trial" (specific, low-friction)
- "See how [Company] reduced churn by 40%" (outcome-specific)
- "Book a 20-minute demo" (specific time commitment)
- "Download the free checklist" (clear deliverable)

**CTA editing checklist:**
- [ ] Does the CTA say what happens after clicking?
- [ ] Is there a friction reducer near the CTA? ("No credit card required," "Cancel anytime")
- [ ] Is the CTA visible without scrolling on mobile?
- [ ] Is there only one primary CTA per section?

## Headline Improvement Formulas

Headlines are the highest-leverage copy on any page. Spend extra time here.

**Weak headline patterns:**
- Product name as headline ("Acme Corp")
- Category claim ("The Best [Category] Software")
- Vague promise ("Grow your business")

**Strong headline patterns:**

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| Specific outcome | "Cut reporting time by 80% — without adding headcount" |
| Problem → solution | "Tired of chasing approvals? Sign-off in one click." |
| For [ICP] | "For ops teams that run on spreadsheets — and hate it" |
| How we do X | "We built the only tool that connects your CRM and billing in real time" |
| Social proof | "10,000 teams ship faster with [Product]" |
| Contrast | "More features than [competitor A]. Cheaper than [competitor B]." |

**Headline test:** Cover the rest of the page. Does the headline alone communicate the core value? If not, rewrite.

## Readability Scoring

**Target readability scores by content type:**
- Landing pages: Flesch-Kincaid grade 7-9 (accessible to most readers)
- Blog posts: Grade 9-11 (slightly more depth OK)
- Technical docs: Grade 11-13 (audience expects complexity)

**Tools:** Hemingway App (free), Readable.com, Microsoft Word's readability stats.

**Quick fix for poor readability:**
1. Break sentences > 25 words into two sentences
2. Replace any word > 4 syllables with a shorter synonym
3. Convert passive constructions to active

## Brand Voice Consistency Check

Before finalizing edits, compare against brand voice guidelines:

| Dimension | Check |
|---|---|
| Tone | Is this formal/informal match correct? |
| Vocabulary | Does it use brand-approved terms? |
| Sentence length | Does it match typical sentence length in brand content? |
| Humor | Is any humor consistent with brand use of humor? |
| Punctuation | Does it match brand conventions (Oxford comma, em-dashes, etc.)? |

If no brand voice guidelines exist, create a 1-page doc defining 5 core attributes before editing.

## Editing Workflow

**For a landing page (full edit):**
1. Read once without edits — understand the whole
2. Clarity pass — fix all passive voice, jargon, complex clauses
3. Benefit pass — convert features to outcomes
4. Cut pass — remove all filler words and phrases
5. Voice pass — humanize; read aloud
6. CTA pass — sharpen every button and call-to-action
7. Headline pass — test 3 alternative headlines before deciding

**For a single piece of copy (quick edit):**
1. Flag passive voice instances
2. Replace jargon with plain language
3. Cut 20% of words (you almost always can)
4. Check the CTA

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|---|---|
| "The original writer will be upset if we cut too much" | Copy editing is not about the writer. It's about the reader. Cut what doesn't work. |
| "Our audience is technical — they want detail" | Technical audiences want clarity above all. Dense copy with jargon reads as low confidence. |
| "We can't simplify — it's a complex product" | Complexity in the product does not require complexity in the copy. Simplify the copy; let the product be complex. |
| "The long headline tests better in our industry" | Maybe. Test it. But the default is: shorter wins. Prove otherwise with data. |
| "We should keep 'world-class' and 'best-in-class' — they sound credible" | These phrases have zero credibility because every company uses them. Replace with a specific proof point. |

## Verification

- [ ] Passive voice removed or minimized
- [ ] All jargon replaced with plain language
- [ ] Features converted to customer outcomes (benefits-first)
- [ ] Filler words and phrases cut
- [ ] Readability score < Grade 10 for landing page copy
- [ ] All CTAs are specific (not "Submit" or "Learn more")
- [ ] Headlines tested against at least 2 alternatives
- [ ] Brand voice consistent throughout
- [ ] Read aloud without stumbling
