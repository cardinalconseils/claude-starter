# Market Archetypes Reference

Eight common market archetypes. Match your market to the nearest archetype to shortcut landscape analysis.

| Archetype | How to Recognize It | Typical Profit Pool Location | Typical White Space Signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Monopoly Consolidating** | One player has 60%+ share and is acquiring the next tier. Pricing power is unchallenged. Customers complain but stay. | The dominant player's core product (often a platform layer) | Verticals or geographies the monopolist can't serve profitably — too small, too specialized, or too regulated |
| **Fragmented Cottage** | No player has >15% share. Hundreds of small operators. Prices are inconsistent. Buyers shop on relationships, not features. | Professional services wrapped around the product (implementation, customization) | A software layer that aggregates the fragmented players or serves the buyer who's tired of managing 5 vendors |
| **Platform War** | 2-3 players competing for developer / merchant / partner lock-in. Network effects building. Switching costs rising fast. | Whoever wins the platform layer — margin extracted from third-party participants | Niche verticals underserved by the general platform; specialized tools that ride the winning platform |
| **Regulatory Moat** | Incumbents protected by licenses, certifications, or compliance costs. New entrants face 12-36 month approval cycles. | The compliance and certification layer (incumbents charge premium for trust signal) | Workflow software that helps buyers navigate compliance without becoming a licensed entity themselves |
| **Network-Effect Race** | Value increases with users. The winner of user count wins the market. Growth rate is the only metric that matters now. | The dominant network (often winner-take-most) | Complementary tools that work alongside the network but don't compete with it; or a vertical network in a segment the general network ignores |
| **Commoditizing Middle** | Core product is becoming a commodity (price compression, open-source alternatives). Margin is migrating to services or adjacent layers. | The services layer above or the platform layer below the commodity product | A better DX (developer experience) or UX on top of the commodity infrastructure; or a managed service that removes the ops burden |
| **Vertical Integrator** | One player owns multiple layers of the stack (e.g., hardware + software + services). Margin extracted by bundling. Buyers pay for convenience, not features. | The bundled offering — individual components sold separately have lower combined margin | Unbundled best-of-breed tools for buyers who don't need the full stack; or a lower-cost integrated stack for buyers who can't afford the incumbent's bundle |
| **Niche-to-Beachhead** | An upstart has won a specific niche with a tailored product. Attempting to expand from that beachhead into adjacent segments. | The niche (high retention, strong product-market fit, pricing power within the segment) | The niche the upstart hasn't reached yet; or a defensive position in the upstart's beachhead if you can outspecialize them |
