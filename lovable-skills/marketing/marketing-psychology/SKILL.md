# Marketing Psychology & Behavioral Science

Expert knowledge for applying psychological principles and behavioral economics to marketing copy, design, and campaigns.

## Cialdini's 7 Principles of Influence

### 1. Reciprocity
People feel obligated to return favors. Give first.

**Applications:** Free resources (lead magnets, templates, calculators); free trials that deliver real value before asking for a credit card; helpful email content before any ask.

### 2. Commitment and Consistency
People act in ways consistent with prior commitments. Get small yeses first.

**Applications:** Quiz or assessment funnels (small investment → completion); free trial → paid trial → annual plan (escalating commitment); interactive calculators where users input their data.

**Key:** Design your funnel as a series of escalating micro-commitments. Each yes makes the next yes easier.

### 3. Social Proof
When uncertain, people look to others' behavior for guidance.

| Type | Power | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Expert endorsement | High | "Recommended by [authority]" |
| Customer testimonials | High | Specific outcome + customer name |
| User counts | Medium | "Join 50,000+ teams" |
| Ratings and reviews | Medium | "4.8/5 from 1,200 reviews" |
| Logo walls | Medium | Customer logo bars |

**Social proof matching:** A mid-market SaaS prospect is more persuaded by a testimonial from a similar company than from a Fortune 500 enterprise. Match proof to ICP identity.

### 4. Authority
People defer to credible experts. Signal expertise.

**Applications:** Author credentials on blog posts; media mentions ("As seen in: [publications]"); data-backed claims ("Based on analysis of 1M+ sessions"); thought leadership content that demonstrates deep expertise.

### 5. Liking
People say yes to people (and brands) they like. Be likable.

**Applications:** Authentic brand voice (not corporate speak); shared values in brand messaging; founder-led content that shows personality; customer spotlights and stories.

### 6. Scarcity
Things that are limited feel more valuable. Create legitimate scarcity.

**Legitimate scarcity:** Limited beta access (genuine capacity limits); limited-time pricing (with a real deadline); limited seats to a live event.

**Warning:** Fake scarcity ("Only 3 left!" when there are unlimited) damages trust when discovered. Use only genuine scarcity.

### 7. Unity
People are more influenced by those they see as part of their group.

**Applications:** "For [specific identity] by [specific identity]" positioning; language that signals insider knowledge; ICP-specific messaging ("Built for growth-stage SaaS teams").

## Kahneman: System 1 and System 2 Thinking

**System 1:** Fast, automatic, emotional, effortless. Pattern recognition. Gut feeling.
**System 2:** Slow, deliberate, rational, effortful. Analysis. Critical thinking.

Most buying decisions are made by System 1 and then justified by System 2.

**Practical:** Design your hero section for System 1 (look, feel, headline clarity). Support with System 2 content below the fold.

## Key Cognitive Biases in Marketing

### Loss Aversion
Losses feel approximately 2× more painful than equivalent gains feel good.

**Applications:**
- "Don't lose your data" > "Save your data"
- "You're currently missing [specific opportunity]"
- Trial expiry warnings ("Your free access ends in 3 days")
- Risk reversal: "Money-back guarantee if you're not satisfied"

### Anchoring
The first number encountered heavily influences subsequent judgments.

**Pricing anchor applications:**
- Show the highest tier first (enterprise plan as the anchor)
- Show annual price before monthly
- Show crossed-out original price next to sale price

### The Framing Effect
How information is presented changes how it's evaluated — even when the facts are identical.

| Equivalent frames | Effect |
|---|---|
| "90% success rate" vs "10% failure rate" | Same fact, different feel |
| "Save 20 hours/month" vs "20 hours saved per month" | Action-oriented vs passive |
| "Only $4/day" vs "$120/month" | Same price, different salience |

### The Decoy Effect
Adding a third option makes one of the other two look better.

**Pricing application:**
| Plan | Price | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Basic | $29/mo | Anchor |
| **Pro (target)** | **$79/mo** | Looks like the smart choice |
| Enterprise | $89/mo | Makes Pro look like a steal |

Enterprise at $89 makes Pro at $79 look like a steal.

### The Zeigarnik Effect
People remember uncompleted tasks better than completed ones and feel compelled to complete them.

**Applications:**
- Onboarding progress bars ("Complete your profile — 60% done")
- "You're almost ready" prompts showing incomplete setup
- Email sequences that reference an unfinished action

### Mere Exposure Effect
Familiarity breeds preference. The more you see something, the more you like it.

**Applications:**
- Retargeting campaigns (repeated exposure builds trust)
- Consistent brand identity across all touchpoints
- Content marketing builds familiarity over months before conversion

## The Behavioral Design Stack

When designing any marketing element, layer these in order:

1. **Default to the desired behavior** (pre-check "Save my settings," pre-select recommended plan)
2. **Reduce friction for desired actions** (fewer form fields, one-click options)
3. **Add friction to undesired actions** (confirm before cancel, explain what they'll lose)
4. **Use social norms** ("Most customers choose..." or "Teams like yours use...")
5. **Make the desired path the obvious path** (visual hierarchy, contrast, white space)

## Ethical Use

These principles work. That's why ethics matter.

**Use psychology to:**
- Help people make decisions that are genuinely good for them
- Remove friction from good choices
- Surface real value they might overlook

**Never use psychology to:**
- Create false urgency or scarcity
- Use dark patterns (hiding unsubscribe, pre-checking paid options)
- Manipulate into purchases they'll regret
- Exploit vulnerable populations

Long-term trust is worth more than any short-term conversion lift from manipulation.

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Our customers are rational — they'll evaluate the facts" | All customers are human. System 1 precedes System 2 in every buying decision. |
| "Adding urgency always helps" | Fake urgency destroys trust when discovered. Use only real scarcity and real deadlines. |
| "Social proof doesn't matter in B2B — it's all rational" | B2B buyers have career risk. Social proof ("trusted by companies like yours") reduces perceived risk. |
| "This is too subtle to make a difference" | Anchoring and framing effects are consistent across thousands of studies. Small copy changes produce measurable conversion differences. |
